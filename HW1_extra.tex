\section*{Problem 1}
A number $x$ is a perfect square if and only if $\exists m \in I$ such that $x = m^2$. Prove that if $x$ and $y$ are both perfect squares, then $xy$ is also a perfect square.

\section*{Problem 2}
If $n = ab$, then $\sqrt{a} \le \sqrt{n}$ or $\sqrt{b} \le \sqrt{n}$.

\section*{Problem 3}
Show that if $x$ and $y$ are rational numbers, then $x + y$ is rational.

\section*{Problem 4}
Show that if $x$ is irrational then $1/x$ is irrational.

\section*{Problem 5}
Use contradiction to show that $r^3 + r + 1 = 0$ has no rational solution. (Hint: assume $r = a/b$ and analyze the parity of $a$ and $b$.)

\section*{Problem 6}
Show that the last digit of any perfect square is either $0, 1, 4, 5, 6,$ or $9$.

\section*{Problem 7}
Given two positive real numbers $x$ and $y$, the arithmetic mean is $\frac{x+y}{2}$ and the geometric mean is $\sqrt{xy}$. Determine which mean is greater and prove the inequality holds for all positive $x,y$.

\section*{Problem 8}
(Engel) Consider a group of $n$ people such that every subset of four people has at least one person who knows the other three. Show that such a group must contain at least one person who knows everyone in the group.
