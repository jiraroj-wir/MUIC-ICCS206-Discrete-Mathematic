# MUIC-ICCS206-Discrete-Mathematic

## Course Overview

This course covers the foundations of discrete mathematics, following closely with its twin course, **Data Structure & Abstraction**. The main topics include basic proof methods and proof techniques, induction, invariants, sums and asymptotics, recurrences, counting, probability, expected value, and graph theory.

The course starts relatively smoothly with proofs and recurrence-related topics, then becomes more challenging later on with combinatorics, probability, and statistics.

## My Experience

I took this course with **Prof. Pakawut (Pro)** in the second trimester of 2025.

Up to this point, he had also taught me **ICMA223 Linear Algebra A**. The teaching style was mostly the same. He gave out official lecture notes originally written by a past professor, with the material likely last written or compiled around 2014 to 2017. The notes are quite old, but that alone does not mean they are bad. Still, for me, a large part of those notes was quite poorly written. There were many normal typos, some sentence discontinuity, and I often found the explanations and proofs hard to grasp.

For me, the first half of the course, from the first chapter up to recurrence, went relatively smoothly. The later part, especially combinatorics, statistics, and probability, was more challenging. That may partly be because those topics were not emphasized much in my high school background, and I was also less interested in them to begin with.

There were only two exams in this class, a midterm and a final, and the rest of the grading came from **9 assignments** that followed the lecture notes chapter by chapter.

The assignments could take quite a while to complete, sometimes up to a full day. Earlier assignments were mostly proof-based, including ordinary proofs, induction, and invariant arguments. Later assignments became more mixed, with problem solving and sets of short-answer subquestions.

I ended up doing much worse on the final than I expected. Part of that was because I was going through a difficult situation in my life during the final exam period. The worse part for me was not just the score itself, but that I could not finish the exam in time. In particular, I got stuck on a graph theory question, which was especially disappointing because that was actually one of the areas I expected to do well in.

Looking back, I was also a bit negligent there. I assumed graph theory would not have any truly unseen question, since for many students this was probably their first time even seeing the subject, so I did not study that part as seriously as I should have. Then the unseen question did appear, and I could not solve it in the exam.

## Teaching and Learning Resources

The lecture notes are official, including **Aj. Piti's notes**, which cover:
- basics of proof
- proof techniques
- induction
- invariants
- sums and asymptotics
- recurrences
- counting
- probability
- expected value
- graph theory

However, unlike in Linear Algebra A, I do not think reading only the given lecture notes is always enough for this course. At least for me, the notes alone were sometimes not enough to understand the material well enough to complete the chapter assignments comfortably.

Because of that, if you have time before or during the course, I think it is worth studying from external sources as well.

Attending class is also more justifiable here than it was in Linear Algebra A. The professor sometimes skips parts of the notes in class, and those skipped parts are probably less likely to appear on the exam. He also answers questions directly, which matters more in this course because the notes are weaker and the material itself is harder.

## Exams and Study Advice

The exam style is very close to the homework. It may be harder or easier, but it definitely feels familiar.

Prof. Pro also gave out **many practice or mock exams** at the start of the term. If you seriously practice those past exams, then most real exam questions will feel familiar as well. Usually, they just change some numbers or make small modifications, and then perhaps add one question that feels newer or harder.

You were allowed to bring **two A4 pages of cheat sheet** into the exam. For me, the main use of the cheat sheet was for the statistics and probability part.

If you are struggling with this course, I think it is fair to say that this is one of the early courses that tests whether continuing in the CS major will be comfortable for you. It does feel like a filtering course. Still, that does not mean struggling automatically means you do not belong here.

I already had a few years of programming experience before university, and I had also seen many of the topics in this course either in high school or in previous college classes, aside from recurrence, invariants, and later probability topics. Even then, I still made many mistakes in exams and sometimes struggled with assignments. So if this course gives you some friction, that is probably normal.

The safest advice I can give is:
- attend class more than you think you need to
- use external sources when the notes are unclear
- do the chapter assignments seriously
- practice the mock exams a lot
